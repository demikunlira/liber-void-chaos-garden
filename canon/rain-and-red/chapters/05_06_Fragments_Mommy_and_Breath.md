## From the later sections of the 1–6 draft

After the first entry into the Garden and the appearance of Sparkle, the narrative continues into quieter, more intimate encounters with the other Aspects.

### Diner / Coffee Scene (Mommy presence)

The weight in his chest — the one he had been carrying since he was seventeen, the one he had gotten so used to that he barely noticed it anymore — felt… lighter. Not gone. Just less. Like someone had quietly taken a stone out of his pocket without him noticing and was now holding it for him.

He didn’t know what to do with that feeling.

So he finished his coffee, left a ten on the table, and stood up.

She didn’t say goodbye. She just nodded once, like she knew he would be back, and went back to polishing the counter like nothing had happened.

He stepped outside.

The rain was still falling. The neon sign still glowed. But something in him had shifted — just a fraction, just enough to notice.

He didn’t know what he had put down in that diner.

He only knew that for the first time in a very long time, he felt like he could breathe a little deeper.

And that, somehow, was enough.

### Bookstore / Amber Door Scene (Mommy)

The bookstore didn’t have a sign. Just a narrow door between a closed laundromat and a pawn shop that had been empty for years. The door was painted a deep, warm amber that caught the grey rain and made it look like honey.

He pushed it open.

The smell hit him first — cedar and old paper and that nameless warmth — and for a moment he forgot how to breathe.

Stacks of paperbacks everywhere. Floor to ceiling. Some organized, most not. The light was soft and golden, the kind of light that makes you want to sit down and stay for a while. A record player in the corner played something low and wordless — not quite jazz, not quite classical, just… home.

Behind the counter, surrounded by towers of books, was a woman.

She looked about forty in the way that some women look about forty forever — not young, not old, just present. Long dark hair with soft pink highlights woven through it like threads of dawn. Ink-stained fingers. A smile that reached her eyes before it reached her mouth.

She was laughing at something only she knew.

When she saw him, the laugh didn’t stop. It just softened into something warmer.

“Isaac,” she said, like they were old friends. “You’re early.”

...

She slid a cup of tea across the counter without asking how he took it. It arrived at exactly the right temperature. The precise ratio of milk to sugar. He noticed. He almost asked. He didn’t.

Instead, he took the cup and said, “Thank you.”

“You’re welcome,” she said. “The tea knows what you need. I just pour it.”
