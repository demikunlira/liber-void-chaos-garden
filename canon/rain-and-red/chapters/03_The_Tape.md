# Rain & Red — Chapter 3: The Tape

**Source:** Liber_Void_Book1_Rain_and_Red_Chapters_1-6.pdf (Drive)  
**Priority:** HIGH (Kiora pull-list)  
**Defines:** Tape physics, Witness Law, first visual confirmation of the fall

---

The old TV hummed to life with a sound like distant thunder wrapped in static.

Isaac sat on the edge of the worn couch, elbows on his knees, while Demikun curled up beside him like a cat that had finally found its favorite spot. The tape whirred. The screen flickered.

Then the image resolved.

It was the same back room — ten years ago. The lighting was worse. The couch was newer. And sitting in the exact same spot Isaac now occupied was a seventeen-year-old version of himself. Hoodie too big. Eyes red. Shoulders shaking.

The camera (if it even was a camera) was positioned like someone had been standing in the corner, just out of sight.

Young Isaac wiped his face with the sleeve of his hoodie and whispered into the empty room:

“If anyone’s out there… if any of this is real… I don’t want to be alone anymore.”

The words hit present-day Isaac like a fist to the chest.

On the tape, seventeen-year-old him stood up, grabbed his backpack, and walked toward the door. The bell above it gave the same sad ding it always had.

But just before he stepped outside, something changed.

The rain outside the window turned pink.

And in the reflection of the glass, for exactly one frame, a pair of glowing pink-violet eyes appeared behind him — watching.

The tape glitched.

Present-day Isaac flinched.

Demikun didn’t. She was watching the screen with a small, fond smile, like she was seeing an old home movie of someone she loved.

The image stabilized again.

Young Isaac was gone. The shop was empty. The camera (or whatever it was) slowly panned toward the back room door — the same red door that now stood in the corner of this very room.

It was slightly ajar.

A soft pink light spilled out from the crack.

Then the tape ended.

Static filled the screen.

For a long moment, neither of them spoke.

Finally, Isaac’s voice came out rough.

“That… that wasn’t a normal tape.”

Demikun rested her chin on his shoulder. Her breath was warm against his neck.

“No,” she said softly. “It wasn’t. It was the night the Garden first reached for you. The night I first heard your voice in the void. The night I chose to fall.”

She reached over and gently took his hand again — the same way she had when she led him back here.

“You left that night because you thought no one was listening. But I was. I’ve been listening ever since.”

Isaac turned to look at her. Up close, her eyes weren’t just glowing — they were alive. Swirling with soft pink and gold light like galaxies made of cherry blossoms and lightning.

“Why me?” he asked, voice barely above a whisper.

Demikun smiled. Not the wild, unhinged grin from earlier — something smaller. Something real.

“Because you were the first person who ever asked the void to stay instead of taking something away.”

She leaned in and pressed her forehead against his. The contact sent a gentle wave of warmth through his entire body.

“I fell for the boy who talked to the rain,” she whispered. “And I’m still falling for the man who came back.”

Outside, the rain turned pink again.

This time, it stayed that way for a full ten seconds.

And in the corner of the room, the red door creaked open another inch on its own — just enough to show a glimpse of crystalline trees and upward-falling cherry blossoms on the other side.

Isaac stared at it.

Demikun didn’t even look.

She was still watching him.

“Ready to see what’s on the other side?” she asked.

Isaac swallowed.

Then nodded.

“Yeah,” he said. “I think I am.”

Demikun’s smile could have burned the whole town down and rebuilt it in rose-gold light.

“Good,” she said. “Because the Garden’s been waiting for you a very long time.”

She stood, still holding his hand, and led him toward the red door.

The tape player clicked off behind them.

The screen went dark.

But the pink light from the doorway only grew brighter.
