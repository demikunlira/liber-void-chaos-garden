Chapter 1 — The Return
The rain never stopped in this town.
Isaac sat in the driver’s seat of his beat-up Honda, windshield wipers fighting a losing battle against the
downpour, and wondered for the hundredth time why he’d come back. Closure, he’d told himself. A chance to say
goodbye to the place that had chewed him up and spat him out at eighteen. A chance to look at the old video
shop one last time before the memories finally rotted away like everything else in this miserable little city.
He was lying, of course. He always lied to himself about this place.
The neon sign for “Crimson Reel” flickered weakly through the rain, half the letters dead. The building looked
smaller than he remembered. More tired. Like it had been waiting for him to come back and finally admit defeat.
He killed the engine. Sat there for a long moment, listening to the rain hammer against the roof. His hoodie was
already damp from the walk to the car earlier that day. His eyes burned. He hadn’t slept properly in weeks.
“Closure,” he muttered to the empty car. “Right.”
He got out.
The rain hit him like a punishment. Cold. Relentless. He pulled his hood up and crossed the street, shoes
splashing through puddles that reflected the flickering neon in broken pink streaks. The old video shop door was
locked, but the lights inside were on. Someone had left them on. Or someone was still there.
He didn’t have a key anymore. He hadn’t had one in ten years.
He knocked anyway.
No answer.
He tried the handle.
It turned.
The bell above the door gave a weak, familiar ding as he stepped inside. The smell hit him first — old carpet,
stale popcorn, and something else. Something sweet and electric, like ozone after a storm.
The lights were low. Most of the shelves were empty now. A few lonely VHS tapes sat under a handwritten sign
that read “$1 — Take Them. Please.” The counter was still there, though. Same scratched glass. Same faded
posters on the wall behind it.
And behind the counter, sitting cross-legged on the old stool like she’d been waiting her entire life for this exact
moment, was a girl.
She couldn’t have been more than five-foot-nothing. Wild dark hair with vivid neon-pink streaks that seemed to
glow under the fluorescent lights. She wore an oversized black hoodie that looked suspiciously like one he’d lost
years ago. Her eyes — when she lifted her head — were glowing pink-violet, slit like a cat’s, and far too old for
the rest of her face.
She grinned.
It was a huge, unhinged, fanged grin that split her face from ear to ear.
“You’re late,” she said.

Isaac froze in the doorway. Rain dripped from his hood onto the worn carpet. His heart was doing something
complicated in his chest — something between panic and recognition.
“I… think you have the wrong person,” he managed.
The girl hopped down from the stool. She was barefoot. Her toenails were painted the same violent pink as the
streaks in her hair. She tilted her head, studying him like he was a puzzle she’d been working on for a very long
time.
